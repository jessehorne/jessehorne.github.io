On Questions

Gone are the days that finding information meant taking a trip to the library. Communication through the Internet has changed all of that. One can search questions and properly formed questions, in return getting massive amounts of links to pages on websites that might just have what you are looking for.

In my experience, it is a combination of "What you ask", "How you ask it", and "Where you ask". Let us look at Case 1.

Bob, the new and eager-to-learn programmer is trying to learn Python. He has bought (or perhaps torrented) a couple books, and got to the chapter on List Comprehensions but can't quite comprehend them. His friend has told him about IRC, so he decides to use the Freenode web client to connect to the Freenode network. He then joins #python.

When Bob joins, he immediately blurts out "Hi, anyone know python?". After a single minute of no response, he says "Anyone here????". I could go off on a rant about proper IRC etiquette, and I will, in another article. Anyhow, you can plainly see that this is "stage 1" of "How you ask". He has shown the others that he is impatient, and has opened his mouth, removing all doubt.

A kind user responds "Hello Bob, I can help you, what is your question?". Bob then asks "How do I make List Comprehensions work?". I will end this case here and explain what is happening and how the situation could have been improved. The new user was too impatient. He could have waited a short while and studied how others here communicate and ask questions. He then goes to ask an unspecifc question relating to the decently sized world of List Comprehensions. He could have asked a better, more specific question. If he were having troubles understanding what exactly a List Comprehension was, and had already spent a good amount of time researching himself, he could then ask something like "I have been struggling to understand what my issue was with learning List Comprehensions. Here is my code *link here*, could anyone check it out and see what I am doing wrong?".

Here is how you properly ask a question in most cases.
1. Research! Spend as much time as possible doing your own research on your issue, before going to others. Some people have too much to do besides attempting to explain complex subjects to people in text or vocal communication.
2. Study your medium. If you are on IRC, get to know how it works, and learn your IRC etiquette. The same goes with anything else, such as Teamspeak, or Skype.
3. Spend several moments formulating a question that makes total sense, and is specific. If you can't understand your question, how is someone else going to?
4. Be patient. You have, if you are smart, already spent a long time on your issue, and deserve a break. Wait for a response.
5. Once (if) your question is answered, be sure to put this new knowledge into practice. If you don't understand the answer, then it looks like you have a new question to formulate.
6. Be sure to thank the person for helping you. Even if someone didn't get the point across, thank them anyway.


Some if not all of this should be common sense, and asking questions is something you learn to do well.
