The Aspiring Professional

by Jesse Horne

Life for the aspiring professional, regardless of the field, can be described simply as a bucket of emotions. I believe that comes with being young, and I've been told that the "chemicals" in my brain will begin to "settle" in my mid-twenties. I am eighteen, at the time of writing this. I am an aspiring intellectual, but to be more specific, I am a young and determined software engineer. Being young in any field brings its own struggles, but I feel the unique experience of being a software developer must be described. So if you are interested to know how it feels to be a young software developer, looking for a place in this world, continue reading.

I decided early on in my life, around the age of twelve, that programming was something that was interesting to me. I was first introduced to programming when aquiring information on how games were developed. More specifically, I wanted to know how Runescape worked. It was the first game I became hooked on. Of course, for someone who had never written the traditional "Hello, World!" application, this was going to be quite the journey. I can't recelect how I discovered the Google search engine, but it definitely saved me a lot of time. I had heard that the traditional means of acquiring information was at the library. The school I attended didn't have the books that I needed, so I took to the web.

I'm not going to describe in full, the journey that got me to where I am today, but I will sum it up as best as I can. I went from Batch Files, to QBASIC, to QB64, to Python, to C++, to Lua, to Python, to C, to C++, to C, to Lua, to Ruby, to many esoteric programming languages, to Lua, and so on. I'm sure there are software engineers out there today that can relate to what I just said. I wasn't the type to commit to a single lanugage, and stick it out till I had gained a mastery understanding. I was the type that picked up the basics of programming by experimenting with various languages, and reading lots of literature regarding the subject, online. After many, many hours spent at my computer, I began to lose the relationship that a "normal" kid should have with his/her family. I believe it would have been like this regardless of the path I took in life, but I digress. After writing my first program, that worked, I was hooked. This is what I wanted to be when I grew up.

Software Engineering is a fairly broad term. A hundred lifetimes would not allow me to learn everything there is to know regarding the subject. That can't prevent the aspiring professional from pursuing their goals! One thing I learned quickly from programming, is that a problem worth pursuing can be broken down into smaller, more manageable sized problems. First, you want to examine your overall goals. Then you need to break them down into smaller goals, or tasks. After this, you work day and night to complete them. How you choose to complete your goals is up to you. I chose the path of obsession. Long days, and nights are a given with that path. I can be very impatient sometimes, and it definitely affects how I go about learning a specific thing. To each their own, though, as it's said. Choose the path you want to choose.

There are many struggles an aspiring professional will have. When I first started, everyone had an opinion on where to start. I learned later on that these were just opinions. One of the biggest issues that I have had, though, is the stress, and worry. Most of these worries has been about how I will survive in this world. I chose not to take the path of traditional education. In my opinion, the right interview can determine if you are the best candidate for the position. The need for a degree varies based on the field, but in most cases a degree just means you are entry level. This is great, but until some common problems are fixed with the educational system in America, I will refuse to say it is the only way. My rebel attitude gives power to my bias.

I will end this writing by giving my thoughts on success. Please note that I am not where I want to be in life, nor do I know where I want to be. I know that success comes from perserverence. If everything were easy, there would be no need for professionals. It's never easy. I try not to look at the possible length of the journey. The advice I can give is to take it one day at a time. Train yourself to be able to handle more each day, as you would if you were working out your body. Even if you do choose the slower, more patient path, know that your time here is limited. Use it wisely.
